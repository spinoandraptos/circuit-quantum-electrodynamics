# Transmon Qubit

The **transmon** is the dominant superconducting qubit architecture. It is a modified
Cooper-pair box shunted by a large capacitance $C_B$, designed to suppress sensitivity
to offset charge noise while retaining anharmonicity.

## Cooper-Pair Box Hamiltonian

Starting from the Josephson circuit, quantise the phase $\hat{\phi}$ and charge $\hat{n}$
(number of Cooper pairs transferred) with $[\hat{n}, e^{i\hat{\phi}}] = e^{i\hat{\phi}}$:

$$
\hat{H}_\text{CPB} = 4E_C(\hat{n} - n_g)^2 - E_J\cos\hat{\phi}
$$

where:

$$
E_C = \frac{e^2}{2(C_J + C_B)}, \qquad E_J = \frac{\Phi_0 I_c}{2\pi}
$$

$n_g = C_g V_g / 2e$ is the dimensionless gate charge and $I_c$ is the critical current.

## Transmon Regime

Adding the shunt capacitor $C_B \gg C_J$ reduces $E_C$ while $E_J$ is tuned independently.
In the limit $E_J \gg E_C$:

$$
\hat{H} \approx -E_J\cos\hat{\phi} + 4E_C\hat{n}^2
$$

Expanding around $\phi = 0$ and quantising as a harmonic oscillator with perturbative
anharmonicity gives the transmon frequency:

$$
\boxed{\omega_q \approx \sqrt{8 E_J E_C} - E_C}
$$

## Anharmonicity

The transmon is not a harmonic oscillator; the energy levels are not equally spaced.
The **anharmonicity** is:

$$
\alpha = \omega_{12} - \omega_{01} \approx -E_C
$$

A typical transmon has $E_C/h \sim 200\,\text{MHz}$ and $E_J/h \sim 20\,\text{GHz}$,
giving $\alpha/2\pi \approx -200\,\text{MHz}$ — large enough to address individual
transitions but small compared to $\omega_q$.

## Charge Dispersion Suppression

The sensitivity to $n_g$ (charge noise) falls exponentially in $E_J/E_C$:

$$
\epsilon_m \propto E_C \left(\frac{E_J}{2E_C}\right)^{m+1/2} e^{-\sqrt{8E_J/E_C}}
$$

At $E_J/E_C \gtrsim 50$, charge dispersion is negligible, which is why transmons
are so much more coherent than charge qubits.
